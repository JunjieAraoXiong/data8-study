Exam radar

The eight things that reliably cost points, ranked by how often they appear × how often they fool people — mined from five real exams with solutions (sp23 & su23 midterms and finals, fa24 final). Each ↳ chip jumps to the matching card above.

1

Histograms are density, not height

The y-axis is percent per unit, so “height = proportion” is a recurring — false — T/F. Percent in a bin = height × width; a head-count needs × n on top. A tall narrow bar can hold less data than a short wide one, and merging two equal-width bins gives height (x + y)/2.

§ 7.2 movie-gross histogram
2

The hypothesis-test chain

Four links, each a separate trap: the null is about the population and says “same distribution — difference is chance” (never about “the sample”); a whole-distribution statistic is TVD = sum(np.abs(a − b)) / 2 because signed differences cancel to ≈ 0; simulate the null with sample_proportions(n, model); and the p-value tail (>= vs <=) must point the same way as the alternative. A p-value is not the chance the null is true.

§ 11.1 Swain v. Alabama§ 11.2 Alameda juries
3

Shuffle ≠ bootstrap

Permutation (A/B) tests shuffle labels without replacement; bootstrap confidence intervals resample rows with replacement — plain tbl.sample(). “Sample with replacement to shuffle labels” shows up as a T/F and it is always false. Bonus fact they test: the chance a given row makes it into a bootstrap resample is 1 − (1 − 1/n)ⁿ ≈ 0.63.

§ 12.1 maternal smoking§ 12.3 Deflategate§ 13.2 the bootstrap
4

Bootstrap CI endpoints — and what 95% means

A c% interval is [percentile((100−c)/2, stats), percentile((100+c)/2, stats)] — 2.5/97.5 for 95%, 10/90 for 80%. The meaning: 95% of intervals built this way capture the parameter — not “95% chance it's in this one.” And the bridge to testing: if 0 falls outside your 95% slope CI, reject the null at the 5% level.

§ 13.3 confidence intervals
5

Regression lives in two unit systems

In standard units the line is simply y = r · x. Back in original units: slope = r · SDy / SDx, intercept = ȳ − slope · x̄. minimize(rmse) finds the same line numerically. Residual plots must be flat and centered at 0 — tilted or off-center plots are impossible for a least-squares fit; a U-shape means the relationship is nonlinear.

§ 15.2 Galton heights§ 15.3 Little Women§ 15.5 residuals
6

Table-method identity traps

group('x', min) takes the min of every column separately and can invent rows that never existed — sort first, then group with first. pivot's first argument becomes the columns. And know your types: column → array, item → value, take → table — item('song') and take('song') are the standard wrong answers.

§ 8.3 cones grid
7

The probability block

“At least one” = 1 − P(none), every time. Independent draws multiply; drawing without replacement turns products into chains with shrinking denominators — the part exams flag as “challenging.” Bayes from a tree: the target path ÷ the sum of every path consistent with the evidence.

§ 9.5 finding probabilities§ 18.1 Bayes by tree
8

CLT and the √n rules

Sample means and sums go normal for large n regardless of the population's shape — sample medians and maxes don't, and “regardless of sample size” is false. A 95% CI of a mean has width ≈ 4 · SD / √n; the worst-case SD of a 0/1 proportion is 0.5 — that's how “width ≤ 5%” becomes n = 1600.
